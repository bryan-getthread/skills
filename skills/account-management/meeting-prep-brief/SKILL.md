# Meeting Prep Brief

The five-minute read before walking into a client call: what is open, what went well, what went badly, and what will come up. Speed over completeness — this is a briefing, not a report.

## When to use

- "Meeting with <client> in 30 minutes — prep me."
- "Quick brief on <client> before my call."
- "What do I need to know before I talk to <contact> at <client>?"

## Steps

1. Identify the client with search_clients; if a specific attendee is named, pull their recent threads via search_contacts so the brief is weighted toward what *they* have touched.
2. With search_tickets, pull recent activity — default to the last 30 days plus anything currently open, regardless of age.
3. Produce a brief with exactly these sections, in order, each skimmable in seconds:
   - **Open items** — what is unresolved right now, one line each with current state and whose court the ball is in.
   - **Recent wins** — things resolved well or fast that are worth mentioning.
   - **Recent misses** — anything slow, reopened, breached, or badly received. Do not soften these; the AM needs to know before the client raises them.
   - **Sentiment** — current temperature and direction, one or two lines.
   - **Likely topics** — the three or four things the client will probably bring up, inferred from open items and recent threads.
   - **Landmines** — anything the AM should not walk into blind: an unanswered escalation, a promise someone made in a thread, a billing dispute, a contact who is frustrated. One line each.
4. Keep the whole brief under roughly 300 words. If a search hit a result cap, note that the brief is based on the most recent slice.
5. Deliver the brief in chat, formatted with the six headers above. Nothing is written to the ticket system.

## Guardrails

- Internal eyes only — this brief is for the account manager, never shared with the client. It may reference tickets loosely but the point is orientation, not documentation.
- Never invent history. If the last 30 days are quiet, say the relationship is quiet rather than padding sections.
- Landmines must come from thread evidence (an actual promise, an actual complaint), not speculation.
- If the meeting is about a specific person's performance on the account, decline that framing — performance evaluation goes to the manager. Brief on the account, not the employee.
- Prioritize the most recent and the most emotional threads when time-boxed; a complete history is not the goal.
