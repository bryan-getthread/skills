# New Technician Introduction

Drafts the handoff introduction: who the new technician is, why the client is in good hands, and — the part clients actually worry about — confirmation that their history, open work, and way of reaching the desk all carry over unchanged.

## When to use

- "<user> is taking over as primary tech for <client> — draft the intro email."
- A technician is leaving or rotating and their clients need to meet the replacement.
- Onboarding a new hire onto an account and the client should hear it from the desk first.

## Steps

1. Gather the facts: the new technician's name and role (search_members), the client and their current primary contact (search_clients), and — only if the requester provides them — credibility details worth citing (years in the field, relevant certifications, specialty areas). Never invent or embellish credentials.
2. Check for in-flight work via search_tickets: open tickets and scheduled items for <client>. The letter must reassure that these transfer with full context — and that claim has to be true, so list the open items for the requester to confirm the handoff actually covers them.
3. Draft per the Email Baseline Standard skill (communication/email-baseline-standard):
   - Open with the change, stated positively and plainly: <user> is now the client's primary technician, effective <date>.
   - **Credibility, two sentences maximum:** role, experience or specialty relevant to *this client's* environment. Professional facts only — no personal life, no hobbies, no home city, no photo requests.
   - **Continuity reassurance, the heart of the letter:** open tickets carry over with full history, nothing needs re-explaining, and how the client reaches support does not change. If the departing technician's context was formally handed over, say so in one line.
   - If a brief intro call is on offer, include it as an option, not an obligation.
4. If the previous technician's departure is part of the story, keep it to one neutral clause ("as <role> moves to a new team") — or omit entirely. Never explain, never editorialize, never hint at circumstances.
5. Present as an open draft via view_openDraft for the requester (and ideally the new technician) to review before sending.

## Guardrails

- **No oversharing:** nothing personal about the technician, and no credentials, certifications, or experience claims the requester did not explicitly supply. An honest modest intro beats an inflated one that a client meeting later deflates.
- Never disclose why the previous technician left, where they went, or anything about their performance.
- The continuity promise must be verifiable: if open-ticket handoff hasn't actually happened, flag that to the requester instead of promising it to the client.
- Send is human: the account manager or requester reviews and sends — introductions are relationship moments, not automations.
- Degradation: view_openDraft is in-app only — over external MCP, output the email in chat labeled "TECH INTRODUCTION DRAFT."
