# De-escalation Reply

Drafts the reply that lowers the temperature: heard, owned, and moving — the three things an angry client is actually asking for.

## When to use

- "The client is furious — help me respond."
- An incoming message is hostile, ALL CAPS, threatening escalation, or dripping with sarcasm.
- Sentiment on a thread has turned sharply negative and the next reply matters.

## Steps

1. Read the full thread first. Anger usually has a history — find the missed follow-up, the repeated issue, or the unmet expectation that lit the fuse. The reply must show we know it.
2. Draft per the Email Baseline Standard skill (communication/email-baseline-standard) with this spine:
   - **Acknowledge** the frustration and the specific experience behind it, in one genuine sentence. Name their actual complaint, not a generic "your frustration."
   - **Own what's ours.** If the record shows we dropped something, say so plainly ("we should have updated you Tuesday, and we didn't"). No excuses attached to the apology.
   - **Concrete next step + time commitment.** Exactly what happens next, who does it, and by when ("I'm looking at this now and will call you before 3pm today"). This line is the de-escalator; without it the rest is noise.
3. Strip every escalation trigger from the draft: no "as previously stated," no "per our policy," no "however," no explaining why their expectation was unreasonable, no matching sarcasm or formality-as-a-weapon.
4. Keep it short — 3–5 sentences. Long defensive replies re-escalate.
5. If the anger involves a threat to cancel, legal language, or an executive on copy, recommend the technician loop in the account manager before sending (see communication/escalation-notice-client if the ticket is being escalated).
6. Present as an open draft via view_openDraft. A human always reviews an angry-client reply before it goes out.

## Guardrails

- **Never match tone.** Calm, warm, specific — regardless of what came in.
- Own only what the ticket record supports; never accept blame for things not established (defensive writing — this thread may be forwarded).
- The time commitment must be one the tech confirms they can keep; ask them before baking it in if unclear. A broken de-escalation promise is worse than none.
- Never promise refunds, credits, or personnel consequences ("we'll deal with the technician").
- Degradation: view_openDraft is in-app only — over external MCP, output the draft in chat labeled "DRAFT — review before sending."
