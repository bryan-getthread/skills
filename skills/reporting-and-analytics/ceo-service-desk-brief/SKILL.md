# CEO Service Desk Brief

Translate the service desk into a business readout for the owner: how the operation is trending, where the risk sits, and the one decision that most needs their attention — written entirely in business language, zero ticket IDs.

## When to use

- "I'm the CEO — review the service desk for me."
- "How is the desk really doing?" from an owner who doesn't live in the queue.
- A monthly owner-level operations brief.

## Steps

1. Confirm the period (default: last 30 days versus the prior 30) and gather the underlying data with split searches per signal per board, excluding junk/NOC boards and auto-closed statuses. Track caps for the methodology note.
2. Distill to business dimensions:
   - **Demand** — is client-driven work growing, flat, or shrinking, and which clients drive the change.
   - **Delivery** — are we keeping up: closure versus intake, backlog direction, responsiveness trend.
   - **Client experience** — sentiment trend and any relationship at risk (multi-signal only, never volume alone).
   - **Operational risk** — capacity pinch points, single-person dependencies, chronic issues without root-cause fixes.
3. Write ≤400 words in plain business English: three trend paragraphs (demand, delivery, experience), one risk paragraph, then **one decision** framed with its options and your recommendation ("hire versus automate the alert triage", "have the call with <client>", "fund the RCA project").
4. Strip all operational identifiers: no ticket IDs, board names, or status jargon. Client names appear only where the CEO must act on that relationship.
5. Append a two-line methodology note: period, data sources, and any result caps (the one place honesty about mechanics belongs in this brief).

## Guardrails

- No ticket IDs anywhere — if a point needs a ticket reference to land, it is too operational for this brief; roll it up.
- Exactly one decision ask. A CEO brief with five asks gets zero decisions.
- Be honest, not promotional: if delivery slipped, say so plainly with the trend evidence; do not soften into meaninglessness.
- Distinguish measured trends from hypotheses; never dress a hunch as data.
- Never flag a client relationship as at-risk on volume alone — require corroborating signals (sentiment, unresolved majors, recurrence).
- Owner's eyes only; contains competitive and client-relationship candor.
