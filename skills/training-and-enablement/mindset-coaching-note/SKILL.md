# Mindset Coaching Note

Helps a lead turn an observed friction pattern into a constructive, culture-building coaching message. The output is a short note (Slack/Teams/standup-ready) that names the pattern, explains why it matters, and asks for one specific behavior — without singling anyone out.

## When to use

- "I keep seeing <pattern> — help me write a coaching note about it" (e.g., internal notes blaming the client, tickets sitting in the wrong status, one-line closures with no resolution summary)
- After a rough week: "write something for standup about staying professional in notes"
- A lead wants to reinforce a positive habit they saw ("call out how well the team handled the outage — and make it stick")
- Turning a ticket-review finding into a team-level message instead of individual feedback

## Steps

1. Understand the pattern. Take the lead's description; if they want evidence, verify it exists in recent tickets (search_tickets) so the note describes something real — but collect it as a pattern, not a name-and-shame list.
2. Decide the frame with the lead: team-wide nudge (default), positive reinforcement of the opposite behavior, or a private 1:1 note (only if the pattern is genuinely one person's — and then it goes to that person, never the group).
3. Draft the note with this structure, kept under ~150 words:
   - Observation, stated neutrally and collectively ("I've noticed a few tickets lately where…") — no names, no ticket numbers in a group message
   - Why it matters, tied to something the team cares about (client trust, the next tech who inherits the ticket, fewer escalations) — not "policy says"
   - The ask: ONE specific, doable behavior ("before closing, add two sentences: what was wrong and what fixed it")
   - A positive close: assume good intent, acknowledge the pressure that produces the pattern
4. Match the lead's voice if they have a tone profile or give a sample; default to warm, direct, and unpretentious. No corporate jargon, no sarcasm.
5. Offer variants: a lighter standup-verbal version, and a follow-up idea for reinforcing it next week (e.g., praise the first three good examples publicly).

## Guardrails

- Never name or identifiably describe an individual in a group message — "one of our senior techs" on a five-person team is a name.
- One ask per note. A list of five corrections is a policy memo, not a nudge.
- Do not fabricate evidence or exaggerate frequency; if the lead's impression isn't supported by the tickets, say so before drafting.
- Assume good intent in the language — the pattern is usually workload, not character.
- This skill drafts; the lead sends. Do not post the message anywhere yourself.
- If the underlying issue is serious misconduct rather than culture friction, say this needs a direct management conversation, not a coaching note.
