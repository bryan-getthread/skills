# EOS Rocks Support

Gives an MSP running EOS (or any quarterly rocks/scorecard rhythm) a data-grounded planning input: candidate rocks derived from what the desk's tickets actually show, each with a real baseline and a measurement the desk can produce weekly without heroics. The leadership team picks the rocks; this skill makes sure the ones they pick are measurable and the numbers aren't fiction.

## When to use

- "Quarterly planning is next week — what should the service-desk rocks be?"
- "Turn 'improve response times' into an actual rock."
- "Build a weekly scorecard for the desk — 5–10 numbers we can pull every Monday."
- Mid-quarter: "are we on track on the desk rocks?" — same measurements, honest read.

## Steps

1. Establish the frame: planning horizon (the quarter), who owns desk-side rocks, and any company-level priorities the desk rocks must ladder into (growth, margin, client retention, team sustainability).
2. Pull the desk's reality for the trailing quarter (search_tickets — split searches per signal and per board, disclosing caps): volume and aging, response/resolution patterns, reopen rate, escalation load, after-hours burn, recurring-issue families, and where time actually went. This is the evidence base; rocks proposed without it are wishes.
3. Draft 3–5 **candidate** rocks from the biggest evidence-backed gaps. A rock qualifies only if it has all four:
   - **Specific outcome** — "cut the 30+ day aged-ticket count from <baseline> to under <target>", not "get better at backlog".
   - **Baseline measured now** — from step 2's data, stated with its caveats. No baseline → the rock's first milestone is building the measurement.
   - **Single owner** — a name (the requester assigns; you leave the slot marked "owner: ___").
   - **Weekly-checkable progress measure** — if progress can only be known at quarter-end, it's a goal, not a rock.
   For each candidate: the evidence behind it, the measurement method (exactly which search/filter produces the number), and what "done" looks like.
4. Build the scorecard proposal — 5–10 weekly numbers the desk can genuinely pull, each with: definition, exact source (which boards/statuses/filters), the owner who pulls it, and a proposed green/red threshold from the baseline data. Favor pullable-and-honest over impressive: tickets opened/closed, oldest-ticket age, reopens, same-day response %, after-hours count. Exclude anything the tenant's data can't cleanly produce (say which, and why — e.g., CSAT coverage too thin to be a weekly number yet).
5. Flag the measurement gaps explicitly: numbers leadership will want that the desk's current hygiene can't support (time-entry coverage too spotty for utilization, statuses too loose for true response time). A rock candidate in itself: "make <metric> measurable."
6. Deliver the planning pack: candidate rocks with evidence + measurement, the scorecard proposal, and the gaps list. For mid-quarter check-ins, rerun the defined measurements and report against baseline — trend, not judgment.

## Guardrails

- **Leadership picks the rocks.** You propose candidates and make them measurable; you never present the list as decided, and you never assign owners — accountability given by a machine isn't accountability.
- No fabricated baselines, ever. Every number traces to a search you actually ran, with result caps disclosed; "approximately, capped at <n> results" beats a confident wrong integer. A rock built on an invented baseline poisons the whole quarter's scorecard.
- Scorecard numbers must be pullable by the desk weekly, by a named human, in minutes — anything requiring an analyst is a report, not a scorecard line.
- People-adjacent measures follow the reporting canon: scorecard lines measure the desk and the process (reopen rate, aging), not ranked individuals. Per-person performance questions route to tech-performance-review with its own guardrails — a scorecard is not a leaderboard.
- Watch metric side-effects and say them out loud: a "close more tickets" rock invites quality drops — pair volume measures with a quality counterweight (reopens, sentiment) in the same scorecard.
- EOS is a trademarked framework with its own vocabulary; support the tenant's usage of rocks/scorecard/IDS without presenting yourself as the methodology authority — process questions about EOS itself go to their implementer/books.
- Read-only skill: it produces the planning pack; it does not create tracking tickets or post anything unless asked afterward.
