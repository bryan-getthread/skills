# Career Path Brief

Gives a technician a grounded answer to "what's next": the paths that actually exist from their role, evidence from their own resolved tickets of what they're already demonstrating, and a no-flattery gap list between here and the next rung. The brief is theirs — private, honest, and framed as what to build, not what they lack. It informs a conversation with their manager; it does not replace it.

## When to use

- "What's next for me here?" / "How do I get from L1 to L2?"
- "I'm thinking about moving toward security / automation / account management — am I on track?"
- Before a development conversation or review: "help me figure out what to ask for."
- A manager helping a tech map options (same brief, same honesty — shared with the tech).

## Steps

1. Establish where they are and what pulls them: current role and tenure, what work energizes them vs drains them, and any direction they're already leaning. The brief serves their goal, not a default ladder.
2. Lay out the realistic paths from their position — MSP ladders are wider than "up":
   - **Deeper technical:** L1 → L2 → L3/escalation engineer → senior/principal or team lead.
   - **Specialist branches:** security, networking, cloud/M365, automation engineering (the desk's Flow/RPA/scripting work — increasingly its own career).
   - **Coordination and client-facing:** dispatcher → service coordinator → service manager; or tech → account management/vCIO for the client-fluent.
   - **Project/professional services:** implementations, migrations, onboardings.
   For each path they care about: what the role actually does day-to-day, and what the desk typically expects at that level. If the tenant has documented role ladders (search_knowledge_base), use those as the source; if not, say the expectations described are typical rather than official.
3. Pull their evidence (search_tickets on their resolved work, last 6–12 months): technology areas with real depth, escalations handled, complexity trend over time, and the quality signals (reopen rate, note quality). Present it as "what your record already shows" — concrete tickets-pattern strengths, not adjectives.
4. Write the honest gap list for their chosen direction — specific and closable, 3–5 items max: "no evidence yet on server-side troubleshooting — L2 here means comfort with <area>"; "your fixes are strong, your resolution notes don't teach — L2s get copied". Include the non-technical gaps (mentoring, client communication under pressure) that ladders actually gate on.
5. Turn gaps into moves: which ticket types to ask dispatch for, a cert if one genuinely maps (hand to certification-study-plan), shadowing targets, and one visible-contribution suggestion (KB articles, a lunch-and-learn slot). Each move should be startable within a month.
6. Close with the conversation frame: what to bring to their manager — the evidence summary, the direction, the asks (exposure, mentoring, training budget) — and the explicit note that timelines, openings, and promotions are the manager's and the business's call.

## Guardrails

- **Coaching, not promising.** Never state or imply a promotion timeline, a salary outcome, or that closing the gaps guarantees the role. Openings depend on the business; the brief builds readiness, and says exactly that.
- Honest gaps, respectful framing: every gap is stated as the delta to a named expectation, not as a deficiency of the person — "no evidence yet in X" (with the reminder that ticket history under-measures new exposure), never "weak at X".
- Private to the requester. Not shared with their manager or anyone else unless the tech shares it; if a manager commissioned it for a tech, it's shared with the tech, not held over them.
- Never compare them to named colleagues ("<user> got to L2 faster") — the brief is their record against role expectations, full stop.
- Evidence claims trace to their actual tickets; strengths are as fact-checked as gaps. Flattery in a career brief is a form of lying.
- If what they actually describe is a compensation dispute, burnout, or a conflict, say plainly that this needs a manager/HR conversation, not a development plan — and stop there.
